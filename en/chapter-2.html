The Psychology of Time

Our Subjective Perception of Time

Time is an objective reality — 24 hours a day for everyone — but our perception of it is deeply subjective. When we are absorbed in an enjoyable activity, time flies; when we are bored or anxious, it seems to drag. This perceptual distortion plays a central role in procrastination.

Psychologists Philip Zimbardo and John Boyd, in their Time Perspective Theory, identified several time orientations: past-focused (nostalgia or regret), present hedonistic (living in the moment), present fatalistic (feeling powerless), and future-oriented (planning and delaying gratification). Procrastinators are often strongly present-hedonistic.

Hyperbolic Discounting — The Temptation of Now

Our brain undervalues future rewards relative to immediate rewards disproportionately — this is hyperbolic discounting. In practice: €100 today feels far more desirable than €110 in a week, even though waiting is objectively more advantageous.

This mechanism explains why we choose to watch a series rather than prepare a report: immediate pleasure is tangible, while the reward of completed work seems distant and abstract. The gap narrows under pressure — which is why so much work gets finished the night before the deadline.

Key concept: Hyperbolic discounting is the tendency to prefer smaller, immediate rewards over larger, future ones. It underlies many procrastination behaviors.

The Planning Fallacy

Described by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky, the planning fallacy is our systematic tendency to underestimate the time and resources needed to complete a task. We plan imagining the ideal scenario, without accounting for unforeseen events, fatigue, or inevitable interruptions.

Studies show that even knowing we typically underestimate, we continue to do so. The solution is not to plan more cautiously, but to adopt the "outside view" — looking at how similar tasks actually went in the past, rather than trusting our optimistic projections.

Time Blindness

Some people, particularly those with ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder), suffer from what psychiatrist Russell Barkley calls "time blindness" — a difficulty perceiving the passage of time and anticipating future deadlines. For them, there is only "now" and "not now," with no gradient between the two.

Even without ADHD, we can all experience episodes of time blindness during intense stress, cognitive overload, or exhaustion.

Flow State and Time Management

Psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi described the state of flow: total concentration where we are so absorbed in an activity that we lose track of time. Flow is the optimal state of productivity and well-being. It occurs when the challenge level of a task exactly matches our skills.

Remember: Our relationship with time is shaped by emotions, cognitive biases, and neurology. Recognizing these mechanisms allows us to consciously outsmart them, rather than be their victims.
